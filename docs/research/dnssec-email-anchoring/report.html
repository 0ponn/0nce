0pon · Technical Report · DNS Security · June 2026

Governments sign their zones.
Almost none anchor their email keys.

A census of US federal .gov domains, a .mil sample, and a thirty-country landscape, testing whether each domain's DNSSEC chain — and the DKIM key that authenticates its email — validates to the DNS root.

Michael Layug, 0pon  ·  June 2026  ·  validation by delv from the IANA root trust anchor

Abstract

The premise — a transatlantic DNSSEC gap disadvantaging the United States — is not supported by the data. US federal civilian DNSSEC adoption is strong (75.4% of 1,343 domains, a full census) and exceeds an international sample of flagship government portals. The genuine deficits lie elsewhere: the US military trails its own civilian agencies, with major commands — including US Cyber Command and DISA, operator of the .mil registry — unsigned; and across every jurisdiction the DKIM email-signing key is almost never anchored to root, because organizations delegate it by CNAME into a mail provider's unsigned zone. The consistent adopters, everywhere, are the national DNS registries.

§1Findings

This study asks a question most email-authentication audits skip. It is well understood whether a domain publishes a DKIM key; it is rarely asked whether that key is itself cryptographically anchored — whether an adversary able to spoof DNS could substitute it. We measured two things from the IANA root trust anchor: whether each zone's DNSSEC chain validates, and whether the DKIM record at selector._domainkey.domain validates as a record in a signed zone rather than a pointer into an unsigned one.

Four results stand out. First, US federal civilian DNSSEC is strong and, if anything, ahead: 75.4% of all 1,343 .gov domains validate to root,1 against roughly 61% of the international flagship-government domains sampled. Second, the military lags the civilian government — and the unsigned set includes the very organizations chartered for cyber defense. Third, the email key is almost never anchored: 4.2% of federal civilian domains, and none of the sampled military domains. Fourth, the reliable adopters are not a region but a profession — the national DNS registries, which sign their zones nearly universally and hold most of the world's root-anchored email keys.

§2Method

The federal .gov set is a complete census of the official CISA/GSA domain list2 (1,343 domains). The .mil set is a curated sample, as the Department of Defense publishes no registry; the international set pairs one flagship government domain per country with the national DNS registry where one was sampled (25 of 31 countries; six are government-only). Validation uses delv3, which resolves and validates from the root anchor independently of any recursive resolver. A DKIM key is counted as anchored only if it is a direct record returning a fully-validated chain — a record reached through a CNAME into an unsigned provider zone does not qualify.

Three measurement artifacts were identified and corrected, each of which would have overstated success: a non-validating resolver in the test environment (the AD flag was unusable, motivating delv); the two-verdict output delv emits for a CNAME chain — fully validated for the signed pointer, then unsigned answer for the key it targets; and unstable verdicts under parallel load. A serial re-check of fifteen domains classified unsigned found no false negatives.

The cyber-mission military is unsigned

In the .mil sample, the following domains are not DNSSEC-signed — each independently verifiable, so the observation does not rest on sample size:

army.mil · navy.mil · af.mil · marines.mil · spaceforce.mil · cybercom.mil · disa.mil · pacom.mil · dla.mil

DISA operates the .mil registry; CYBERCOM is US Cyber Command. Defense email runs on DoD365, which delegates DKIM by CNAME into *.onmicrosoft.us — unsigned — so no sampled military key anchors to root.

Where federal civilian email keys are anchored, they concentrate among the organizations handling the most sensitive correspondence: the intelligence community (cia.gov, ic.gov, odci.gov) and the national laboratories (lbl.gov, ameslab.gov, nnss.gov), alongside a few high-traffic services (noaa.gov, gps.gov, gsa.gov). Competence tracks consequence.

§4The international landscape

The cross-country picture dissolves the regional framing entirely. National DNS registries sign their zones almost everywhere (23 of 25 sampled), a near-universal professional norm. Flagship government portals are far more uneven — and the unsigned set is not where one would guess.

Email-key anchoring is rarer still, and concentrated almost entirely in registries — DENIC, AFNIC, nic.it, nic.ch, dns.pt — with only two governments in the sample, Denmark and Spain, self-hosting a signed key. Everyone else delegates the key into an unsigned tenant.

§5Discussion

A signed zone with a delegated key is a half-measure that reads as protection. The zone's cryptographic guarantees stop at the CNAME; the key that actually authenticates the mail inherits the security of unauthenticated DNS to a third party. The remediation is unglamorous and concrete: publish the DKIM key as a signed record within one's own DNSSEC zone, not as a pointer into a provider's unsigned tenant.

The finding also bounds the near-term value of any system that proves DKIM-key authenticity from DNSSEC — a zero-knowledge DNSSEC proof can attest only to a key that is anchored to begin with, and that precondition is presently rare: roughly four percent of federal civilian domains, none of the sampled military, and outside the registries, vanishing. The same census yields the small set of domains where such a proof is demonstrable today.

Limitations. The absolute civilian figure (75.4%) is roughly double the last published academic measurement (~38%, September 2023)4; the most plausible reading is genuine, mandate-driven growth, but it should be treated as a June-2026 snapshot by this method, with the NIST deployment monitor5 as the authoritative reference. DKIM presence was probed through roughly ten common selectors without a mail corpus and is therefore undercounted; international coverage is one flagship domain per country. A single snapshot.
