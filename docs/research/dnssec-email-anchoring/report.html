DNS Security Census · 0pon · June 2026

Governments sign their zones. Almost none anchor their email keys.

A full census of all 1,343 US federal .gov domains, a .mil sample, and a ~31-country international landscape — measuring whether each domain's DNSSEC chain, and its DKIM email-signing key, actually validate to the DNS root.

Method: independent validation from the IANA root anchor (delv). Scope: .gov = full census; .mil & international = samples.

Bottom line

The hypothesis was a US-vs-EU DNSSEC deficit. The data refutes it. US federal civilian DNSSEC is strong — and ahead of the international flagship-government sample. The real divide is operator culture, not geography: DNS registries do it right everywhere; governments and senders mostly don't. The US-specific weak spot is the military, and email-key anchoring is missing worldwide.

The finding that should sting: the cyber-mission military isn't signed

In the .mil sample (~47% signed overall), these are not DNSSEC-signed — each individually verifiable, so it holds regardless of sample size:

DISA operates the .mil TLD. CYBERCOM is US Cyber Command. Both unsigned. DoD email runs DoD365 → CNAME into *.onmicrosoft.us (unsigned), so 0 sampled .mil keys anchor to root.

Who does it right in .gov

The 56 root-anchored domains skew to the intelligence community (cia.gov · ic.gov · odci.gov) and national labs (lbl.gov · ameslab.gov · nnss.gov), plus noaa.gov · gps.gov · gsa.gov. The most sensitive-mail orgs self-host signed keys.

Signed zone, unanchored key — the universal failure

Email keys that actually validate to root are almost all registries — nic.ch · nic.cz · denic.de · afnic.fr · nic.it · dns.pt · traficom.fi · internetnz.nz — plus two governments, 🇩🇰 borger.dk and 🇪🇸 lamoncloa.gob.es. Everyone else CNAMEs DKIM into an unsigned provider zone (Microsoft 365, SendGrid, Mailchimp; DoD365). The cryptographic identity of government email is, in effect, only as trustworthy as unauthenticated DNS to a third party.

Is this reliable?

Yes — method validated, and three traps that each would have over-reported success were caught before these numbers:

  1. Controls pass: delv validates a known-good signed domain and rejects a deliberately-broken one.
  2. Spot-check clean: 15 random "unsigned" .gov re-validated serially → 0 timeout artifacts; 5/5 "signed" reconfirmed.
  3. Traps fixed: a non-validating test resolver; delv's two-verdict CNAME output; parallel-load false positives → only direct, fully-validated keys count as anchored.

Calibration: our 75.4% is ~2× the last published academic figure (~38%, Sept 2023) — likely real mandate-driven growth, but treat the absolute as a June-2026 snapshot (NIST's monitor is authoritative). Limits: international coverage is uneven — one gov domain per country, a registry for 25 of ~31 (per-domain facts, not country rankings); DKIM detection used ~10 guessed selectors (undercounts presence); single snapshot.

Why it matters

A zero-knowledge DNSSEC proof can let a verifier confirm an email key is authentically published — but only if the key is anchored to root to begin with. This census quantifies how rare that precondition is (~4% US civilian, ~0% military, ~18% of sampled international infra) and names a concrete ask: publish your DKIM key as a signed record in your own DNSSEC zone, not a CNAME into an unsigned tenant. Validating test vectors fall out for free: gsa.gov · cia.gov · noaa.gov · denic.de · afnic.fr.
