Governments sign their zones. Almost none anchor their email keys.

Findings

The premise was a transatlantic DNSSEC gap leaving the US behind. The data refutes it — and points somewhere more interesting.

Most email-authentication audits ask only whether a domain publishes a DKIM key. The harder question is whether that key is itself cryptographically anchored — whether an adversary who can spoof DNS could simply substitute it. Measuring validation from the root anchor, four results stand out.

US federal civilian DNSSEC is strong — 75.4% of all 1,343 .gov domains validate to root, above the ~61% of international flagship government domains sampled. The military lags its own civilian government, and the unsigned set includes the organizations chartered for cyber defense. The email key is almost never anchored — 4.2% of civilian domains, none of the sampled military. And the consistent adopters are a profession, not a region: the national DNS registries, which sign their zones nearly universally and hold most of the world's root-anchored email keys.

Method

The federal .gov set is a complete census of the official CISA/GSA list2 (1,343 domains). The .mil set is a sample — Defense publishes no registry — and the international set pairs one flagship government domain per country with the national DNS registry where one was sampled (25 of 31 countries; six government-only). Validation uses delv3, which validates from the root key independently of any resolver. A DKIM key counts as anchored only when it is a direct record returning a fully-validated chain; a key reached through a CNAME into an unsigned provider zone does not.

Three measurement artifacts were caught and corrected, each of which would have overstated success: a non-validating test resolver, the two-verdict output delv gives for CNAME chains, and unstable verdicts under parallel load. A serial re-check of fifteen unsigned-classified domains found zero false negatives.

The cyber-mission military is unsigned

These .mil domains are not DNSSEC-signed — each individually verifiable, so the point holds regardless of sample size:

army.mil · navy.mil · af.mil · marines.mil · spaceforce.mil · cybercom.mil · disa.mil · pacom.mil · dla.mil

DISA operates the .mil registry; CYBERCOM is US Cyber Command. Defense email runs on DoD365, which delegates DKIM by CNAME into *.onmicrosoft.us — unsigned — so no sampled military key anchors to root.

Where civilian keys are anchored, they cluster among the organizations handling the most sensitive mail: the intelligence community (cia.gov, ic.gov, odci.gov) and the national laboratories (lbl.gov, ameslab.gov, nnss.gov), with a few high-traffic services (noaa.gov, gps.gov, gsa.gov). Competence tracks consequence.

The international landscape

The cross-country picture dissolves the regional framing. Registries sign their zones almost everywhere — 23 of 25 sampled — a near-universal professional norm. Flagship government portals are far more uneven, and the unsigned set is not where one would guess.

Email-key anchoring is rarer still, concentrated almost entirely in registries — DENIC, AFNIC, nic.it, nic.ch, dns.pt — with only two sampled governments, Denmark and Spain, self-hosting a signed key.

Discussion

A signed zone with a delegated key is a half-measure that reads as protection. The zone's guarantees stop at the CNAME; the key that actually authenticates the mail inherits the security of unauthenticated DNS to a third party. The remediation is unglamorous and concrete: publish the DKIM key as a signed record inside one's own DNSSEC zone, not as a pointer into a provider's unsigned tenant.

The result also bounds the near-term value of any system that proves DKIM-key authenticity from DNSSEC: such a proof can attest only to a key that is already anchored, and that precondition is presently rare — roughly four percent of federal civilian domains, none of the sampled military, and outside the registries, vanishing. The same census names the small set of domains where such a proof is demonstrable today.

Limitations. The civilian figure (75.4%) is ~2× the last published measurement (~38%, Sept 2023)4 — likely real mandate-driven growth, but a June-2026 snapshot by this method, with the NIST monitor5 as authoritative reference. DKIM presence was probed via ~10 common selectors and is undercounted; international coverage is one flagship domain per country; a single snapshot.
